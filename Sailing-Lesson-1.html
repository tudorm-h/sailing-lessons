The #1 Rule

On a boat, ropes are called LINES.

Each line has a specific name based on its job.

If you know the name, you know the job!

The Mainsail
The primary and most important sail
What is it?

The large triangular sail attached to the mast and boom. It's your primary power source.

Why it matters

The mainsail is raised first and taken down last. On the Seawind 1600, it has a full-batten design for a clean shape.

Parts of the mainsail
  • Head — top corner (halyard attaches here)
  • Tack — front bottom corner (at the mast/boom)
  • Clew — back bottom corner (outhaul attaches here)
Main Halyard
The line that raises the mainsail

What It Does

  • Hoists (pulls up) the mainsail to the top of the mast
  • Runs from the head of the sail, up through a sheave at the masthead, then back down to the cockpit
  • Once raised, it's cleated (secured) tight

Word Origin

"Halyard" comes from "haul yard"

In the old days, sailors hauled the yard (the horizontal spar) up the mast.

HAL-yard = "HAUL it up the YARD (mast)"
Main Sheet
Controls the boom angle — NOT a sheet of sail!
What it does

Controls the angle of the boom (and mainsail) relative to the boat.

How it works
  • Pull in (sheet in) = boom closer to centreline = more power upwind
  • Let out (ease) = boom swings out = less power, better for downwind
Seawind 1600

Uses a traveller system to fine-tune the boom angle across the boat.

Common Mistake!

"Sheet" does NOT mean a sheet of sail. It's the LINE that controls the sail angle.

Outhaul
Controls mainsail shape and fullness
What it does

Pulls the foot (bottom edge) of the mainsail along the boom towards the end.

How to use it
  • Tight outhaul = flatter sail = better in strong wind (less power, less heeling)
  • Loose outhaul = fuller (baggier) sail = more power in light wind
"OUT-haul" = hauls the sail OUT along the boom
Leech Line
The small line that stops sail flutter
What it does

A thin line running through the trailing edge (leech) of the sail. Tightening it reduces flapping/flutter along the back of the sail.

When to adjust
  • If you hear a rhythmic flapping along the sail's back edge, tighten the leech line slightly
  • Don't over-tighten — it cups the sail and hurts performance

Sail Edge Vocabulary

Reefing Lines
Reduce sail area when the wind picks up
What is reefing?

Reducing the area of the mainsail to handle stronger winds safely.

How it works
  • The line pulls a lower section of the sail down to the boom
  • This shortens the sail, reducing power and making the boat more manageable
Seawind 1600

Typically has single-line reefing — one line does the job from the cockpit.

"Reef early, shake it out later." If you're thinking about reefing, you should already be doing it!
The Preventer
CRITICAL SAFETY LINE
What it does
  • A line rigged from the boom forward to a cleat on the foredeck
  • Prevents the boom from swinging violently across the boat during an accidental gybe
When to use it
  • Always rig a preventer when sailing downwind
  • An uncontrolled gybe can cause serious injury or damage

DANGER

An accidental gybe swings the boom across at high speed. It can knock a person overboard or cause head injuries.

The Jib
The headsail that works with the main
What is it?

The smaller sail set in front of the mast, attached to the forestay.

Why it matters
  • Works with the mainsail to create efficient airflow (the "slot effect")
  • Helps the boat point higher into the wind
  • On the Seawind 1600, it's roller-furling — wraps around the forestay
Key difference from mainsail

No boom! The jib is controlled entirely by its sheets.

Jib Sheets
Two lines, one job — control the jib angle

How They Work

  • Two sheets — one on each side (port & starboard)
  • Only ONE is active at a time
  • The working sheet is on the leeward (downwind) side
  • When you tack, you release one and pull in the other

Tacking Procedure

  • 1. Release the loaded (windward) sheet
  • 2. Helm turns the boat through the wind
  • 3. Pull in the new leeward sheet
  • 4. Trim to correct angle
Jib Furling Line
Roll it up, roll it out
What it does

Wraps the jib around the forestay using a roller-furling drum at the base.

To furl (roll up)

Ease the jib sheet slightly, then pull the furling line — the sail wraps neatly around the stay.

To unfurl (deploy)

Pull the jib sheet while easing the furling line — the sail unrolls.

Partial furling

You can partially furl the jib to reduce its area in stronger winds (like reefing the main).

The Screecher
Your light-wind secret weapon
What is it?

A large, lightweight headsail — bigger than the jib, made from lighter cloth. Set on its own dedicated furler, separate from the jib.

When to use it
  • Light to moderate winds, especially reaching (wind from the side)
  • It catches more wind than the jib when conditions are gentle
When NOT to use it

Strong winds — the screecher is too large and light, it can be overpowered and damaged.

Screecher Lines
Sheets and furling — same concept as the jib

Screecher Sheets

  • Two sheets, port and starboard (just like the jib)
  • Control the angle of the screecher
  • Generally eased more than jib sheets — the screecher likes to fly free

Screecher Furling Line

  • Rolls the screecher up on its own dedicated furler
  • Works the same as jib furling — ease sheet, pull furling line
  • Always furl before the wind gets too strong!
Hoisting Into Wind
Always point into the wind when raising sails

Less Load

Wind flows over both sides equally, so the sail doesn't fill with power while you're raising it.

Safer

No sudden forces on the sail, lines, or crew. The boat stays stable and manageable.

Easier

The sail slides up freely without catching the wind. Fighting a filled sail is exhausting!
