The Three Sails

  • Mainsail — the big boss sail
  • Jib — the helper sail up front
  • Screecher — the light-wind supercharger

The Lines (Ropes) We'll Learn

  • Halyards, sheets & furling lines
  • Outhaul, leech line, reef lines
  • Preventer — super important safety line!
Rule #1 — Remember This!

On a boat, ropes are called LINES.

Every line has its own name — and the name tells you what it does!

Learn the name = know the job. Easy!

The Mainsail
The biggest and most important sail on the boat
What is it?

The big sail behind the mast. On our Seawind 1600, it has a cool square top instead of a pointy triangle — this gives us more power!

Why it matters

It's your main engine! You raise it first when you go sailing, and take it down last when you're done.

Parts to know
  • Head — the top (where the halyard clips on)
  • Tack — front bottom corner
  • Clew — back bottom corner
Main Halyard
The line that pulls the mainsail UP the mast

What It Does

Think of it like a flagpole rope — you pull it to raise the sail to the top of the mast!

  • Clips onto the top of the sail (the "head")
  • Goes up through a pulley at the top of the mast
  • Comes back down to the cockpit where you pull it
  • Once the sail is up, you lock the halyard tight

Cool Word Origin!

"Halyard" comes from "HAUL YARD"

Old-time sailors used to haul (pull) the yard (a big pole) up the mast. The word stuck!

Memory trick: HAL-yard = "HAUL it up!"
Main Sheet
Controls the boom angle — NOT a bed sheet!
What it does

Controls how far the boom swings out. Think of it like a dog lead for the boom!

  • Pull it in = boom comes to the middle = more power going upwind
  • Let it out = boom swings out to the side = less power, good for downwind

Don't Get Tricked!

A "sheet" is NOT a sheet of sail! It's the LINE that controls the sail angle. Weird, right?

Outhaul
Makes the sail flatter or fatter
What it does

Pulls the bottom of the sail along the boom. It changes the shape of the sail!

  • Pull it tight = flat sail = good when it's blowing hard
  • Let it loose = baggy sail = catches more wind when it's gentle
"OUT-haul" = hauls the sail OUT along the boom. The name IS the job!
Leech Line
Stops that annoying flapping sound!
What it does

A tiny line inside the back edge of the sail. When the sail flaps at the back, you tighten this line to stop it.

When to use it

Hear a "flap flap flap" from the back of the sail? Tighten the leech line a tiny bit. But don't overdo it — too tight and the sail gets cupped like a spoon!

Sail Edge Names

Reefing Lines
Making the sail smaller when it gets really windy
What is reefing?

Imagine rolling up your sleeves when it's too hot. Reefing is like rolling up part of the sail when it's too windy!

How it works

The reefing line pulls the bottom of the sail down to the boom, making it shorter. Less sail = less power = easier to handle!

Seawind 1600

Ours has single-line reefing — one pull from the cockpit does it all.

"If you're thinking about reefing, you should already be doing it!" — Every sailor ever
The Preventer
SUPER IMPORTANT SAFETY LINE!
What it does

A line tied from the end of the boom to the front of the boat. It stops the boom from swinging across suddenly (called a "gybe").

Why it's so important

If the wind catches the sail wrong, the boom can slam across the boat REALLY fast. That's super dangerous — it can seriously hurt someone.

ALWAYS use the preventer when sailing downwind. No exceptions!

DANGER ZONE

A swinging boom can knock someone into the water or cause a head injury. The preventer keeps everyone safe!

The Jib
The helper sail at the front of the boat
What is it?

A smaller sail in front of the mast, attached to the forestay (the wire going from the mast to the front).

Why we use it
  • Works as a team with the mainsail — together they go faster!
  • The gap between jib and main creates a turbo effect called the "slot"
  • Helps the boat sail closer to the wind
Big difference from the main

No boom! The jib just hangs from the forestay and is controlled by lines called sheets.

Jib Sheets
Two lines, but you only use one at a time!

How They Work

  • Two sheets — one on each side of the boat
  • Only ONE works at a time!
  • The working sheet is always on the downwind side
  • When you turn the boat (tack), you swap sheets

Tacking = Sheet Swap!

  • 1. Let go of the old sheet
  • 2. Boat turns through the wind
  • 3. Pull in the new sheet on the other side
  • 4. Trim it to the right angle
Jib Furling Line
Roll it up like a window blind!
What it does

Think of a roller blind on a window. The furling line wraps the jib around the forestay — rolling it up neatly!

To roll up (furl)

Loosen the jib sheet a bit, then pull the furling line. The sail wraps up like a rolled-up poster.

To unroll (unfurl)

Pull the jib sheet while letting the furling line out. The sail unrolls and catches the wind!

Half-way trick

You can partly furl the jib to make it smaller in strong wind — like reefing, but for the jib!

The Screecher
Your secret weapon for light wind days!
What is it?

A HUGE, super-light sail — way bigger than the jib! It's made from thin material and catches loads of wind on gentle days.

When to use it
  • Light or gentle wind days
  • When the wind comes from the side (called "reaching")
  • It has its own furler on the bowsprit (the pole sticking out the front)
When NOT to use it

Strong wind = BAD for the screecher! It's too big and too thin — strong wind can rip it.

Screecher Lines
Same idea as the jib — sheets and a furling line

Screecher Sheets

  • Two sheets — port and starboard (just like the jib!)
  • Control the angle of the screecher
  • Usually let out more than jib sheets — the screecher likes to fly loose and free

Screecher Furling Line

  • Rolls it up on its own furler on the bowsprit
  • Same trick as the jib — ease sheet, pull furling line
  • Roll it up BEFORE the wind gets too strong!
Hoisting Into Wind
ALWAYS point into the wind when putting sails up!

Less Work

When you face the wind, the sail flaps loosely. It doesn't fill with power, so it slides up easily!

Safer

No sudden pulling on the lines. The boat stays calm and steady while you work.

Way Easier!

Try pulling up a sail that's full of wind — it's like trying to fold a sheet in a hurricane! Point into the wind = easy mode.
